Ordinance Lifecycle by Era

Confirmed, inherited, bulk-ratified, or repealed — each ordinance's fate

Bangladesh's constitution (Art. 93) allows the executive to promulgate ordinances when parliament is not in session; those ordinances must then be placed before parliament. This chart shows the fate of all 396 ordinances across four eras. Converted to Act (teal) = parliament passed an equivalent Act. Repealed / Rejected (red) = removed without parliamentary ratification.

F3

Post-Uprising 2024+ Ordinances

The 2026 parliament's verdict on all 133 interim-government ordinances

The interim government installed after the July 2024 popular uprising promulgated 133 ordinances before the elected parliament convened in 2026. A special parliamentary committee (chaired by BNP's Joynal Abedin) reviewed all 133 against a constitutional April 10, 2026 deadline. 113 confirmed as Acts — 98 as-is, 15 passed with amendments. 4 formally repealed (বাতিল/রহিত) by parliamentary vote: the Parliament Secretariat Ordinance 2024 and the three Supreme Court judiciary-independence ordinances (Judge Appointment; Court Secretariat; its 2026 Amendment). 16 lapsed automatically at the April 10 deadline — parliament did not place them as Bills, so they expired under Art. 93 of the Constitution. The 16 include the Referendum Ordinance, Enforced Disappearance Prevention Ordinance, National Human Rights Commission Ordinances (2024–25), Revenue Policy Ordinances, ACC Amendment, Civil Aviation Amendment, Travel Agency Amendment, Microfinance Bank Ordinance, and Right to Information Amendment. Source: BBC Bangla, 9 April 2026. Hover each slice for details.

Part G

Legislative Drafting Linguistics

G1

Modal Verb Ecology

shall / may / must per 1,000 words by decade — English corpus

Legal language is organised around obligation. The three modal verbs encode different legal weights: 'shall' (mandatory duty), 'may' (permissive authority), 'must' (modern mandatory, avoiding 'shall' ambiguity). Bangladesh's statute book is heavily shall-dominated across all eras — averaging 11.9 'shall' per 1,000 words in English laws and 19.3 in Bengali laws. The UK and Australian plain-language movements (2000s) actively replaced 'shall' with 'must', but Bangladesh's drafting culture never adopted this reform. 'Must' remains near-zero across the corpus — Bangladesh legislators have yet to make the distinction. The 1990s dip in 'shall' coincides with the democratic transition and a wave of procedural laws that favour 'may'-phrased empowerment clauses over mandatory duty-imposition. Theoretical grounding: Garner (2001) Legal Writing in Plain English; Tiersma (1999) Legal Language.

G2

Archaic Legal Vocabulary Index

Colonial-era legal fossils persist across 220 years — and Bengali laws are MORE archaic than English

Archaic legal vocabulary — herein, aforesaid, thereof, whatsoever, deem to be, pursuant to (English) and উক্ত, তদ্কর্তৃক, অতঃপর, তদনুযায়ী (Bengali) — is counted per 1,000 words for each era. The headline finding: Bengali-language laws (gold) show significantly higher archaism (11.4/1k) than their English counterparts (4.6/1k). Rather than modernising the drafting style, post-1987 Bengali drafters transplanted the colonial register wholesale into the new script. The Post-Uprising era shows the lowest English archaism (2.9/1k), suggesting some plain-language improvement in recent drafting. However, Post-Uprising Bengali laws spike to 14.8/1k — the highest of any era — possibly because many were drafted by officials trained in older traditions. Grounding: Tiersma (1999); Crystal & Davy (1969) Investigating English Style; Cao (2007) Translating Law on how translation amplifies rather than reduces archaic register.

G3

Conditional Clause Density by Policy Domain

Land & Property and Local Government laws are the most conditionally complex

Conditional clauses — if, unless, subject to, notwithstanding, provided that, save where — are the primary mechanism through which legislation creates exceptions, qualifications, and dependencies. Counted per 1,000 words across all laws in each policy domain. Land & Property (8.3/1k) and Local Government (8.2/1k) lead all domains — reflecting the exceptionally complex property-rights and administrative-boundary structures these laws regulate. Criminal Law & Justice (7.5/1k) is third — its conditional density reflects the elaborate statutory carve-outs around jurisdiction, mens rea, and defences. Finance, Banking & Tax (4.1/1k) is lowest, perhaps counterintuitively — much fiscal complexity is handled through subsidiary rules and schedules rather than inline conditionals. Grounding: Bhatia (1993) Analysing Genre on conditional structure as a defining feature of legislative texts.

G4

Nominalization Density (English Statutes)

Abstract nouns (-tion/-ment/-ance/-ity) as a legal-genre marker across 220 years

Nominalization — converting verb actions into abstract nouns — is a signature of formal legal register: 'determine' becomes 'determination', 'enforce' becomes 'enforcement', 'eligible' becomes 'eligibility'. Bhatia (1993) identified nominalization as one of the three defining macro-features of Commonwealth legislative genre (alongside post-modification and passive voice). The nominalization index counts all words with abstract-noun suffixes (-tion/-sion/-ment/-ance/-ence/-ity/-ness/-ure/-ship) per 1,000 words. The metric runs at 60–110/1k throughout the corpus — roughly one nominalized form for every 10–15 words. Colonial-era laws (1850–1950s) peak highest, consistent with Victorian administrative language. Modern laws (2009–24) show a modest decline, but the values remain far above general-prose norms (~30–40/1k), confirming that no plain-language reform has displaced nominalization as Bangladesh's default drafting mode.

G5

Passive Voice Density by Era

Agency concealment through the passive voice — sustained across 220 years

Legal drafting is famously passive: 'the application shall be submitted' rather than 'you must submit'. The passive voice removes the subject-agent from view, creating apparent objectivity and avoiding direct address. Passive constructions are counted per 1,000 words for both English and Bengali corpora across eras. The English corpus averages 16.4 passive constructions per 1,000 words — approximately one passive every 3–4 sentences. Early Bangladesh (1971–90) English laws show the highest density, coinciding with the large volume of presidential orders and martial-law ordinances from that era, which are characteristically impersonal in tone. Bengali-language laws also show high passive density, which maps onto the Bengali legal passive pattern করা হইবে / দেওয়া হবে (literally 'will be done/given'). The Post-Uprising era shows some reduction, consistent with a reported shift toward more direct administrative drafting. Grounding: Maley (1994) 'The language of legislation'; Tiersma (1999) on passive as a legal-register feature.

G6

Chained Proviso Intensity by Policy Domain

1,179 'provided further that' chains across the corpus — where do they concentrate?

A 'provided further that' (or 'provided also that') clause is a qualification layered onto an already-qualified provision. The UK Renton Committee (1975) The Preparation of Legislation identified chained provisos as a primary source of statutory obscurity in Commonwealth drafting — yet Bangladesh's statute book contains 1,179 such chains across 2,305 laws. This chart shows the average number of chained provisos per law within each policy domain. Finance, Banking & Tax and Education & Research laws carry the most chains per law, reflecting the dense exception-structures those domains require. Chained provisos appear across all eras: the colonial period's 109, the Pakistan era's 89, Early Bangladesh's 218, Democratic era's 278, and Modern era's 410 — rising in absolute terms as the statute book grows, with no sign of drafting-reform reduction. Grounding: Maley (1994); Bhatia (1993); Renton Committee (1975); Driedger (1982) The Construction of Statutes on the proviso as a drafting pathology.

Part H

Amendment Journeys

H1

Amendment Journeys

How 6 landmark laws changed across political regimes — hover each event for the key driver

Each row is a law; each dot is an amendment event. Circle = Parliamentary Act · Diamond = Executive Ordinance · Square = Presidential Order. Dot colour shows which government enacted the amendment. Background shading marks political eras.

Bangladesh Telecommunication Regulation Act (2001): enacted under BNP to liberalise telecoms; amended in 2006 to add surveillance powers before the election; the Awami League used an Ordinance in 2009 and then a full Act in 2010 for the Digital Bangladesh 3G rollout; the Post-Uprising interim government repealed internet-shutdown provisions by Ordinance in 2025, and the 2026 parliament confirmed those reforms and added net-neutrality provisions.

Int'l Crimes (Tribunals) Act (1973): Mujib enacted it for 1971 war crimes, but trials never completed before his 1975 assassination; dormant for 50 years; then four successive post-uprising Ordinances (2024–25) activated and expanded the tribunal to try Hasina-regime officials for the 2024 student massacre.

Upazila Parishad Act (1998): AL decentralisation vision; restored with elections in 2022 but kept MP budget-control, undermining genuine autonomy; post-uprising Ordinances (2024, 2025) removed the MP-control clause and restructured local finance.

Bangladesh Energy Regulatory Commission Act (2003): BNP market reform; three AL-era amendments progressively weakened BERC independence, culminating in the 2023 'secret LNG' clause; reversed by the 2024 post-uprising Ordinance.

Code of Criminal Procedure (1898): 127-year-old British colonial code, amended only in 2025–26 — three instruments in 18 months — for democratic reforms and digital-era criminal procedure.

Representation of the People Order (1972): independence-era election rules; AL 2023 amendment accused of entrenchment; post-uprising (2025) reformed constituency delimitation and commission independence.
